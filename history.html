The history of crochet.

From humble origins to a global craft — explore how crochet evolved through centuries of creativity and culture.

Origins & Early History

Crochet originated in the 19th century from chain-stitch embroidery worked with a hook instead of a needle. In the late 1840s it was introduced into Ireland as a famine relief measure — centred in Cork in the south and at Clones in County Monaghan in the north. As the craft developed it began to approximate lace, with antique styles such as gros point de Venise being successfully imitated.

Through the Centuries
◆ Early Origins

Ancient Methods & Foundations

Crochet may be derived from early needlework involving shepherd's knitting, linen weaving, lace-making, and embroidery — traditions spanning multiple continents.

Ancient

Ancient Crochet Methods

Derived from early needlework involving shepherd's knitting, weaving of linen, lace-making, and embroidery.

Technique

Tambour Embroidery

A small hook forming chain stitches over fabric — spreading from North Africa and Persia into Europe in the 1700s.

◯ 19th Century

Shepherd's Knitting & European Spread

In the 19th century, crochet became especially popular in Europe, adopted as a mannerly hobby by higher-class women for lace collars, cuffs, doileys, and bedspreads.

Origin

Shepherd's Knitting

Coined early in the 19th century — referring to simple, practical items that sheep herders made in their spare time.

Spread

European Influence

Popularized through emigrants from France, Italy, and Ireland in the early 19th century.

▶ Modern Era

Industrialization & Contemporary Trends

Mass publication of crochet patterns made the craft more accessible, while online communities now share techniques and patterns globally.

Industry

Industrialization

The 19th and 20th centuries promoted crochet as both a practical craft and fashionable pastime through mass-published patterns.

Today

Contemporary Trends

Modern crochet includes amigurumi — crocheted stuffed creatures — and thriving online communities sharing techniques worldwide.

Cultural Significance

Crochet is deeply cultural and practiced across the world — passed from generation to generation as a heritage contributor and source of economic support. It creates social connections and community identity through local gatherings and craft circles, and has been used in social movements as a means of activism and artistic expression. In essence, crochet connects cultural heritage, social impact, and innovation in artistic imagination, reflecting the rich diversity of its global practitioners.
